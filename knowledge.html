Mechanical Design of Prosthetic Hands & Fingers

Objective: To mimic natural hand and finger movement through lightweight, adaptable, and low-cost mechanical systems.

Key Challenges

  • Size, weight, and power constraints
  • Balancing dexterity with mechanical simplicity
  • Ensuring robustness, durability, and user comfort

Actuation & Joint Design

  • Underactuation is common: fewer actuators than degrees of freedom (DoFs), using springs or linkages to adapt to object shapes.
  • Linkage systems (four-bar, slider-crank, whiffle-tree) are widely used for finger flexion/extension.
  • Tendon-driven systems offer compactness but can introduce friction and elasticity.

Finger & Hand Mechanisms

  • Commercial hands (e.g., iLimb, Bebionic, Michelangelo) use rigid or adaptive coupling between MCP and PIP joints.
  • Research prototypes explore underactuation, self-adaptation, and modular designs for partial-hand prosthetics.
  • Thumb design is critical: often includes circumduction for grasp switching (lateral vs. palmar).

Materials & Fabrication

  • 3D printing (PLA, ABS) enables low-cost, customizable prosthetics.
  • Nylon threads and fishing lines act as tendons for finger actuation.
  • PLA is favored for biocompatibility and ease of printing.

Key Innovations

  • Shape-adaptive fingers that conform to object geometry
  • Modular and open-source designs for accessibility and customization
  • Integration of force feedback to prevent object damage during grasping

Outcome: Modern prosthetic hands are becoming more functional, affordable, and user-friendly through advances in underactuated mechanisms, 3D printing, and adaptive designâ€”moving closer to natural hand performance.

Materials

Metals

  • Titanium: Preferred for load-bearing structures; lightweight, strong, corrosion-resistant, biocompatible, and has an elasticity similar to bone.
  • Aluminum & Steel: Used in alloys or plating for strength and lightness.
  • Magnesium & Copper: Historically used; now mainly in alloys for specific applications.

Polymers & Plastics

  • Polyethylene: Flexible, waterproof, used in larger components.
  • Polyurethane (PU): Soft, pliable, used in joints and flexible parts.
  • PVC: Durable coating, but requires stabilizers for heat/light exposure.
  • Silicone: Biocompatible, used in liners and sockets; promotes skin growth and comfort.
  • Acrylics & Polyester Resins: Used in laminations and sockets for durability and adjustability.

Composite & Advanced Materials

  • Carbon Fiber: High strength-to-weight ratio, stiff, lightweight; used in pylons and structural components. Can be brittle and costly.
  • Fiberglass: Reinforces laminations, improves durability, often used with resins.
  • Ionomeric Polymer Metal Composites (IPMC): Electro-active, flexible, used in actuation systems for responsive movement.

Supporting & Interface Materials

  • Spenco, Poron, Nylon-reinforced Silicone: Used in interfaces to reduce friction and improve comfort.
  • Pelite & PET-G: Compressible foams and thermoplastics for socket liners and structural layers.
  • Leather & Fabric: Used in straps, belts, and socks for comfort and moisture management.

3D-Printing Materials

  • ABS & PLA: Most common in Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) for prototyping and low-cost prostheses.
  • Nylon (SLS) & Photopolymers (SLA/Polyjet): Used for more durable, detailed, or flexible parts.
  • NinjaFlex & Filaflex: Flexible filaments for soft, grippy components.

Key Material Considerations

  • Biocompatibility: Must be safe for long-term skin contact.
  • Weight & Strength: Light yet durable enough for daily activities.
  • Water Resistance: Important for durability and hygiene.
  • Cost & Accessibility: Especially critical for developing countries.

Future & Emerging Materials

  • Research focuses on localized, low-cost alloys for developing regions.
  • Smart materials with sensory feedback and self-healing properties.
  • Hybrid composites combining flexibility, strength, and biocompatibility.

Electronics

The electronics systems across the three prosthetic research projects focus on sensing, actuation, control, and energy management, enabling intuitive and functional device operation.

Sensing

  • Surface Electromyography (EMG) sensors detect muscle activity for myoelectric control, providing voltage signals that are amplified, rectified, and filtered to drive actuators.
  • Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs) track residual limb motion, capturing accelerometer and gyroscope data to model natural movement synergies for prosthetic wrist control.
  • Linear potentiometers provide feedback on actuator stroke displacement, enabling closed-loop position control.

Actuation

  • Linear actuators (e.g., Actuonix PQ 12-P) drive tendon-spring mechanisms for finger flexion and extension, offering precise stroke control with non-backdrivable mechanisms to maintain grip without continuous power.
  • DC motors and servo motors are used in tendon-driven and linkage-based systems for both hand and wrist movement.

Control Systems

  • Microcontrollers (Arduino Nano) implement real-time control algorithms developed in MATLAB/Simulink for finger grip patterns and energy-saving strategies.
  • Machine learning modelsâ€”including neural networks and random forest regressorsâ€”decode user intent from IMU or EMG data and predict angular velocities for multi-degree-of-freedom wrist control.
  • Feedback control uses on-off and PID strategies to minimize steady-state error and reduce power consumption.

Power Management

  • Rechargeable Li-Ion batteries (e.g., 18650 cells) power actuators, sensors, and control boards.
  • Energy-efficient control algorithms deactivate motors when positional error is within tolerance, significantly extending battery life during continuous use.

Signal Processing & Communication

  • Low-pass filtering removes noise from EMG and IMU signals.
  • Motor drivers (e.g., L293D) manage actuator direction and speed.
  • Real-time embedded systems run at sampling rates up to 50â€“148 Hz, ensuring responsive and natural prosthetic movement.

These electronic components and strategies collectively enable low-cost, responsive, and user-adaptive prosthetic systems suitable for daily living activities.

User Experience

Key Challenges

  • Trunk Movement: Increased forward displacement of the shoulder (acromion), especially for distant objects.
  • Altered Joint Kinematics: Greater elbow and shoulder range of motion compared to the non-amputated side.
  • Hand Orientation Adjustments: Prosthetic hand orientation is less adaptable, requiring postural adjustments to grasp different objects.

Rehabilitation & Adaptation

  • Phases of Rehab: Includes pre-amputation, post-operative, pre-prosthetic training, prosthetic training, and lifelong care.
  • Training Emphasis: Focus on residual limb care, pain management, functional retraining, and psychological support.
  • Peer & Psychological Support: Critical for adjustment, motivation, and long-term prosthesis acceptance.

Embodiment & Acceptance

  • Embodiment Score: Higher embodiment correlates with shorter reaching times and better integration of the prosthesis.
  • Personalization Needed: UX improves when prosthetic control adapts to individual movement patterns and compensatory behaviors.

Key Challenges

  • High Cognitive Load: Controlling myoelectric prostheses is non-intuitive, sequential, and requires continuous visual attention.
  • Lack of Sensory Feedback: Users rely heavily on vision due to limited tactile or proprioceptive feedback from current devices.
  • Motor Adaptation & Compensations: Users develop compensatory movements (trunk flexion, shoulder abduction) to overcome prosthesis limitations, which can lead to musculoskeletal pain.
  • Prosthesis Abandonment: Common due to discomfort, poor cosmesis, lack of functional benefit, or difficulty in use.

Future UX Directions

  • Intuitive Control Systems: Use of machine learning (e.g., neural networks) to adapt control in real-time.
  • Sensory Feedback Integration: Provide tactile or proprioceptive cues to reduce visual dependency.
  • Ergonomic Design: Reduce compensatory movements through better wrist/hand adaptability and lighter, more responsive devices.
  • User-Centered Training: Virtual reality and gamification to enhance engagement and motor learning.
